02 · Introduction

Tech hiring is different.

Companies care less about your GPA and more about what you can show. A working portfolio, a public GitHub, and one warm referral will beat a hundred cold applications.

This guide walks you through what to do and in what order — starting from zero and ending at your first offer.

03 · Step-by-Step

The 8-step playbook.

Each step starts with one strong action. Do them in order. Don't skip ahead.

  1. 01

    Pick one language and commit.

    Choose Python or JavaScript. Stick with it for three months. Depth beats breadth at the start.

    Screenshot: language docs or your editor
  2. 02

    Build something real every two weeks.

    Tutorials don't count. Ship tiny projects: a calculator, a to-do app, a weather page. Real shipped work proves more than any certificate.

    Screenshot: one of your live projects
  3. 03

    Choose a path.

    Frontend, backend, data, mobile, security, or DevOps. Pick one. You can switch later. A focus makes projects, applications, and interviews easier.

  4. 04

    Create a portfolio site.

    Build one page that shows three to five of your best projects. Include a screenshot, a one-line description, and links to the demo and code.

    Screenshot: portfolio homepage example
  5. 05

    Push everything to GitHub.

    Make your work public. Hiring managers look. Aim for a green contribution graph that shows steady activity over months.

    Screenshot: GitHub contribution graph
  6. 06

    Reach out with intention.

    Send 5 thoughtful messages a week on LinkedIn — to people who do the work you want. Don't ask for a job. Ask for a 15-minute conversation about their path.

  7. 07

    Practice technical interviews.

    Use LeetCode for algorithms. Use Pramp or interviewing.io for mock interviews. Do three full mocks before your first real one.

    Screenshot: LeetCode or Pramp dashboard
  8. 08

    Track every application and iterate.

    Keep a spreadsheet: company, role, date, result, feedback. Treat each rejection as data. Adjust your résumé and pitch each round.

    Screenshot: your application tracker spreadsheet

04 · Conclusion

Getting hired is a process — not a moment.

Skill matters, but consistency and visibility matter more. Show your work. Talk to people. Apply with care. Iterate.

Most students who follow this guide land their first tech role within six to twelve months.

05 · Tips for Success

Five habits that compound.

①

Ship weekly.

A small thing finished beats a big thing planned.

②

Read code.

Open-source projects are the best textbooks. Pick one and read it.

③

Find a mentor.

One monthly conversation with someone two steps ahead changes your trajectory.

④

Compare to past you.

Don't measure against seniors. Measure against yourself six months ago.

⑤

Sleep, eat, move.

Burnout ends more careers than bad code ever will. Take care of the body running the brain.
